\begin{abstract}
Recent research in Artificial Intelligence (AI) is shifting from isolated model scaling toward the design of robust, open-ended, system-level capabilities. In this setting, progress depends not only on larger models, but also on stronger evaluation protocols, architectural control, training-time regularization, retrieval, and agentic workflow design. This paper argues that reliable open-ended AI systems emerge from the coordination of three ingredients: high-fidelity evaluation of generative behavior, mechanisms that stabilize information flow and generalization, and structured multi-agent processes for planning, verification, and revision. Rather than narrating recent advances as disconnected technical improvements, the paper develops a unified argument about why modern AI systems succeed or fail under long-horizon, open-ended workloads. The central claim is that the field is moving from model-centric optimization to system-centric design, and that this transition provides a better framework for understanding both capability growth and persistent brittleness.
\end{abstract}
